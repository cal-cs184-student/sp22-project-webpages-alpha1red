Part 1: Mirror and Glass Materials

When the ray depth is zero, we do not see anything in the room aside from the light. We only care about the direct emission from the light in this instance.

The ray depth being one leads to both spheres being dark, but you can see the walls and floor. The ceiling, aside from the light, is fully dark. Here, we only look at one ray from the light, so this makes sense. We don't take into account the spheres reflecting light.

When the ray depth is two, one sphere starts to reflect, while the other is mostly dark. The sphere on the right is only refractive, so it cannot refract yet, since that takes a ray depth of three. We also see that the mini sphere and ceiling on the left sphere is dark. This is again because the ray depth is two.

When the ray depth is three, the sphere on the right starts to refract. The ceiling on the sphere on the left is no longer dark, but the sphere within the sphere is still dark. We also start to see a glass shadow from the right sphere. This is a result of the right sphere starting to refract.

When the ray depth is four, the mini sphere on the sphere on the right starts to show color, and we see a bit of light on the blue wall to the right of the right sphere. This is due to one extra depth allowing some light rays to bounce onto the wall, as well as onto the mini-sphere inside the left sphere.

The images with max ray depth 5 and 100 do not change all that much, only being slightly brighter and brighter. We are taking into account more ray reflections, so this is natural.
